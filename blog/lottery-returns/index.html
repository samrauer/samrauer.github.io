When does playing the lottery yield a positive expected return?

With a recent powerball jackpot of 1.8 billion, I was curious at what value jackpot the lottery has a positive expected return.

Tickets are $2 + tax = $2.12 in Michigan (6% sales tax). For a positive expected return from playing the lottery, we want the expected value of our ticket to be greater than $2.12.

The expected value of the ticket is

E[R]=∑i=1NR(i)P(i)\mathbb{E}[R] = \sum_{i=1}^{N} R(i) P(i)E[R]=i=1∑N​R(i)P(i)

where

  • R(i)R(i)R(i) is the return value in scenario iii
  • P(i)P(i)P(i) is the probability of scenario iii occurring
  • i=1…Ni = 1\dots Ni=1…N is every possible scenario

There are 69 numbered white balls and 26 numbered red balls. A player chooses 5 white numbers and 1 red number. The white numbers are chosen without replacement.

The more numbers matched with the drawn numbers, the higher the prize. If all white numbers and red numbers are chosen correctly, you win the grand prize which varies depending on the current cash pool.

Let GGG be the grand prize cash take-home value. The expected value of a ticket is

E[R]=G292,201,338+0.3199\mathbb{E}[R] = \frac{G}{292,201,338} + 0.3199E[R]=292,201,338G​+0.3199

We want E[R]>2.12\mathbb{E}[R] > 2.12E[R]>2.12, so solving for GGG we get

G292,201,338+0.3199>2.12⇒G>525,997,984.28\frac{G}{292,201,338} + 0.3199 > 2.12 \quad \Rightarrow \quad G > 525,997,984.28292,201,338G​+0.3199>2.12⇒G>525,997,984.28

This means the grand prize must be at least $526 million before we get a positive expected return from playing the lottery. However, there are a couple factors not included that will reduce our take-home prize amount including

  • state and federal taxes
  • number of grand prize winners
  • most commonly chosen numbers

For federal taxes, 24% is automatically withheld but winning the lottery will probably push you into the highest tax bracket of ~37% (source). For state taxes, Michigan takes an additional 4.25%. This brings the total tax rate to 41.25%, meaning we now need the grand prize to be

G⋅(1−0.4125)>525,997,984.28⇒G>895,315,717.92G \cdot (1 - 0.4125) > 525,997,984.28 \quad \Rightarrow \quad G > 895,315,717.92G⋅(1−0.4125)>525,997,984.28⇒G>895,315,717.92

Even with the jackpot sitting at $1.8 billion, the estimated cash take-home value is only $826 million.

Now, we have to take into account the possibility of not being the only jackpot winner. If multiple people win the jackpot, the winnings are split evenly between all jackpot winners. We will do this by computing the probabilities of number of jackpot winners for a given pot size.

Let

  • ppp be the probability of winning the jackpot
  • nnn be the number of tickets sold
  • XXX be the number of winning tickets random variable

The number winning lottery tickets is distributed as a Binomial distribution

X∼binomial(n,p)X \sim \text{binomial}(n,p)X∼binomial(n,p)

meaning the probability of having kkk winning lottery tickets is

P(X=k)=(nk)pk(1−p)n−kP(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n- k}P(X=k)=(kn​)pk(1−p)n−k

Let Gshared(k)G_{\text{shared}}(k)Gshared​(k) be the take-home value after splitting GGG with kkk other winners,

Gshared(k)=11+kGG_{\text{shared}}(k) = \frac{1}{1+k} GGshared​(k)=1+k1​G

Given you already have a winning lottery ticket, we want the expected value of Gshared(k)G_{\text{shared}}(k)Gshared​(k) over all possible kkk. This can be computed by summing over each kkk,

EX[Gshared(X)]=∑k=0nGshared(k)⋅P(X=k),\mathbb{E}_X[G_{\text{shared}}(X)] = \sum_{k=0}^{n} G_{\text{shared}}(k) \cdot P(X=k),EX​[Gshared​(X)]=k=0∑n​Gshared​(k)⋅P(X=k),

however this is kinda ugly and requires a lot of operations. To simplify this, we can approximate the binomial distribution as a Poisson distribution,

X∼binomial(n,p)→X∼poisson(λ),X \sim \text{binomial} (n, p) \quad \rightarrow \quad X \sim \text{poisson} (\lambda),X∼binomial(n,p)→X∼poisson(λ),

where λ=np\lambda = n pλ=np. This approximation is good when nnn is very large and ppp is very small, so this fits our case nicely. For a more detailed explanation of this approximation, see this video by Steve Brunton. The formula for the Poisson distribution is

poisson(X=k)=λkk!e−λ\text{poisson}(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} e^{- \lambda}poisson(X=k)=k!λk​e−λ

Swapping this into the expectation and doing some math, we get

EX[Gshared(X)]=G∑k=0∞11+kλkk!e−λ=Ge−λλ∑k=0∞λk+1(k+1)!=Ge−λλ∑k=1∞λkk!=Ge−λλ(eλ−1)=G1−e−λλ\begin{align*} \mathbb{E}_X[G_{\text{shared}}(X)] &= G \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{1+k} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} e^{- \lambda} \\ &= G \frac{e^{- \lambda}}{\lambda} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{\lambda^{k+1}}{(k+1)!} \\ &= G \frac{e^{- \lambda}}{\lambda} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \\ &= G \frac{e^{- \lambda}}{\lambda} ( e^\lambda - 1 ) \\ &= G \frac{1 - e^{-\lambda}}{\lambda} \end{align*}EX​[Gshared​(X)]​=Gk=0∑∞​1+k1​k!λk​e−λ=Gλe−λ​k=0∑∞​(k+1)!λk+1​=Gλe−λ​k=1∑∞​k!λk​=Gλe−λ​(eλ−1)=Gλ1−e−λ​​

At this point, starting from a range of cash prizes, we can compute the number of ticket buyers, n(G)n(G)n(G), compute λ\lambdaλ for the Poisson distribution, and compute EX[Gshared(X)]\mathbb{E}_X[G_{\text{shared}}(X)]EX​[Gshared​(X)]. This is done below in a short MATLAB script.

% Expected jackpot earnings of a winning lottery ticket

% jackpot cash prize sizes (in millions)
G = linspace(0.5, 800, 1000);

% number of tickets bought
n = 3170.*(G).^2 + 43454.*(G) + 14063282;

% probability of winning the jackpot
p = 1 / 292201338;

% lambda = n*p
lmbda = n .* p;

% expected shared jackpot (in millions)
G_shared = G .* (1 - exp(-lmbda)) ./ lmbda;
[G_shared_max, idx] = max(G_shared);
G_max = G(idx);


figure(1);
clf(1);
plot(G, n/1e6);
xlabel("Jackpot cash prize (in millions)");
ylabel("# tickets sold (in millions)");

figure(2);
clf(2);
plot(G, G_shared);
xlabel("Jackpot cash prize (in millions)");
ylabel("Expected cash prize after sharing (in millions)");

The expected value of the shared jackpot peaks when the take-home prize is about 340 million, resulting in an expected value of 186 million. Unfortunately, this number is much smaller than our required $895 million to break even, so we do not have a positive return from playing the lottery, even with the optimal jackpot size 😔.

Although, who can really put a price on the thrill of imagining yourself a multi-millionaire by tomorrow morning?

The last thing to think about is when people choose their own numbers instead of using easy pick, they favor smaller numbers. This means if you choose smaller numbers, you are more likely to share your jackpot with someone else compared to if you had chosen larger numbers.
